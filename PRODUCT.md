# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Hiring managers at AI companies. They're scanning dozens of portfolios back-to-back, most of which look the same. They want to feel, within 10 seconds, that someone here has opinions and taste. They're not just evaluating work history — they're evaluating how Akshay thinks about products and whether they'd want to work with him. Context: likely on a laptop, during a focused hiring review session or a casual browse between meetings.

## Product Purpose

Akshay Maru's personal portfolio. It exists to get him hired at an AI company. Success means a hiring manager closes the tab thinking "I need to talk to this person" — not because of the metrics listed, but because the portfolio itself feels like a product decision. The AI chat is not a gimmick: it's the demo. If it works well, it shows product sense. If it's forgettable, it undercuts the claim.

## Brand Personality

Curious, human, warm. Not a resume with animations. Not a tech showcase with a name attached. A person who builds things and cares about the people using them. The voice is direct and specific. The design has point of view but doesn't shout.

## Anti-references

- Over-designed and flashy: particle backgrounds, heavy glassmorphism, excessive animation, trying too hard. The signal is discomfort, not confidence.
- Generic SaaS portfolios: dark navy + neon gradient hero, identical icon-grid cards, Vercel template energy.

## Design Principles

1. **Practice what you preach.** The portfolio is itself an AI product. If the AI chat experience is mediocre, the whole premise collapses. Every design decision should reflect the same standard Akshay applies to what he builds.
2. **Show, don't tell.** Concrete over vague. A specific thing built, a real user count, a decision made — not "results-driven" or "cross-functional." The design should also show restraint rather than tell you it's minimal.
3. **Human over impressive.** Warmth and curiosity beat polish and scale. A hiring manager at an AI company has seen a hundred "5000 users" portfolios. A person who explains why they made a decision is rarer.
4. **Confident restraint.** Knowing when to stop is the signal. The design earns trust by not trying too hard. Subtract before adding.
5. **Specificity is credibility.** Vague claims cost nothing and signal nothing. Every metric, every project description, every heading should be specific enough that someone couldn't have written it without actually doing the work.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA as a baseline. Minimal motion by default — keep animation subtle regardless of the user's `prefers-reduced-motion` setting. Motion should never carry meaning; it's atmosphere only.
